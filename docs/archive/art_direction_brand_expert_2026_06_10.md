# Art direction — brand strategy lens

**Date:** 2026-06-10
**Lens:** Premium women's wellness brand strategy (Goop / Cuyana / Aesop / Nécessaire / Cap Beauty / The Tot)
**Question:** Is Direction A — real-photo collage hero + coquette sticker accent — the right premium register for JeniFit at $47.99/yr?
**Verdict in one line:** Yes, but only with three load-bearing guardrails. Without them, it slips into TikTok-mood-board cheap inside a build cycle.

---

## 1. Where JeniFit sits in the premium register

The wellness-app price ladder, mapped by *brand register* rather than feature set:

```
CHEAP / utility ─────────────────────────────────────── PREMIUM / ritual
$0–$5/mo               $5–$10/mo            $10–$20/mo          $20+/mo + retail
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Noom        Cal AI         BetterMe          JeniFit goal     her75      Equilibria        Goop / Aesop
MyFitnessPal               Future            Bace             Sculpt     The Class         Cuyana
LoseIt                     Cult/Centr         Society         Melissa Wood      Nécessaire
                                              MWH             (post-rebrand)
```

**Where competitors actually sit by register:**

- **Cal AI** — clinical-utility cheap. Greyscale system-sans, gradient-grey button, thumbs-up icons, no warmth. Premium signal: 0. (Reads "I solve a math problem for you.") Conversion machine, but feels disposable.
- **BetterMe** — ad-coded warm-mass. Cream + brown + cartoon mascot icons + 1990s-Reader's-Digest broccoli illustrations. Better than Cal AI on warmth, worse on restraint. Reads like a $9.99/mo direct-response product, which it is.
- **JeniFit target zone** — between her75 and Equilibria. Wants the editorial restraint of her75 but with proprietary warmth (italic Fraunces + cream + cocoa) that prevents her75's slight coldness.
- **her75** — premium editorial. Real-photo polaroid collages, restrained type (one serif italic accent), generous whitespace, hard offset shadows on cards, single hero shot per screen. The clearest visual benchmark in this category.
- **Equilibria / Goop / Aesop** — luxury wellness. Single-object hero, museum-caption typography, warm-not-bright lighting, concrete materials (linen, ceramic, wood), zero stickers, zero exclamation, zero "✨".

**What pushes JeniFit toward premium:**
- Italic Fraunces accent on punch word (already locked, brand signal)
- Cream `bgPrimary` as ONLY background (already locked, anti-grey)
- Cocoa text + thin marks (already locked)
- Scrapbook chrome — 24pt corners, 1.5pt accent border, hard offset shadow (already shipped on Home, Settings, Becoming)
- Negative space confidence (the "Personalizing your space" screen is the most premium one in the her75 set — and it's just type + a progress bar in 60% empty canvas)
- Lowercase casual + 3-month frame (Fraunces does the lifting; sentence content stays grounded)

**What slips toward cheap:**
- AI-generated 3D plastic stickers (the founder already killed these — correct call)
- Multi-emoji clusters (✨🌸💕)
- Pink-on-pink-on-pink (Pink Pilates Princess slop)
- Gradient buttons, especially purple→pink
- Sentence-case marketing polish ("Start your journey today!")
- Stock photography (especially diverse-stock-women-laughing-with-salad)
- Cartoon-flat icon sets (BetterMe broccoli)
- "Most popular" green tag with checkmark (mass-market funnel marker)
- Any "✨" or sparkle decoration
- Heart-as-bullet decoration ("♥ Stay consistent ♥")

## 2. What makes a wellness app feel premium at $48/year

Ten concrete brand cues, in the Cuyana / Aesop / Nécessaire vocabulary:

1. **Negative space confidence.** Premium brands let the canvas breathe. her75's "Personalizing your space" screen is 80% cream. Aesop product pages are 70% white. Cheap brands fear empty space and fill it with stickers, sparkles, and bullet-pointed value props. *JeniFit rule: any screen with more than 4 visual elements above the fold needs a justification.*

2. **Single-object hero shots.** One ceramic mug, one folded linen towel, one bouquet — never a montage. her75 nails this (one polaroid of one woman, full bleed). Cheap brands ship 4-up grids of feature thumbnails. *JeniFit rule: hero beats use 1 photo, never 4.*

3. **Warm-not-bright lighting.** Aesop, Cuyana, The Tot all shoot in warm overcast or golden-hour-without-flare. Cheap wellness shoots in clinical white softbox (Cal AI) or oversaturated Instagram filter (BetterMe). *JeniFit rule: any sourced or shot photo must read as natural light, ideally with film grain.*

4. **Concrete materials over abstract gradients.** Premium = linen, ceramic, oak, paper, brushed brass, raw cotton. Cheap = purple-to-pink gradient buttons, glassmorphism panels, neon glow auras. *JeniFit rule: textures should look touchable. No gradient fills inside cards.*

5. **Specific small objects over generic "wellness" props.** Cuyana shoots a specific tote on a specific Tuesday. Goop shoots one Diptyque candle, not a candle category. The lily bouquet in her75 IMG_6275 works because it's *that bouquet on that day*, not stock "florals." Cheap = stock smoothie, stock yoga mat, stock kettlebell-with-water-bottle. *JeniFit rule: photos must look like one woman's specific Tuesday, not a category.*

6. **Editorial typography over UI typography.** Premium brands use serif display (Fraunces, Canela, GT Sectra), italic-as-emphasis, generous tracking on small caps. her75 is master class. Cal AI is the inverse — pure system sans, hierarchy via weight only. *JeniFit rule: italic Fraunces is the brand signal. Use it, but only on the punch word, never the whole line.*

7. **Restraint in color.** Cuyana ships 3 colors per season. Aesop ships in amber bottles. The premium signal is *limited palette, deeply used.* Cream + cocoa + sage + one accent is luxurious; cream + cocoa + sage + 4 sticker-pack accents is craft fair. *JeniFit rule: any screen should be expressible in 3 colors plus photo.*

8. **Thin refined marks over chunky filled icons.** Premium = 1.5pt stroke, hairline rules, museum-style numerals. Cheap = filled emoji, drop-shadowed buttons, beveled chrome. BetterMe's circle-icon-with-cartoon-illustration row is the cheap pattern. *JeniFit rule: row icons should be 1.5pt strokes or omitted entirely. The thin two-line settings mark is the correct register.*

9. **Subject-as-aspiration, not subject-as-instruction.** Premium photography shows the *life around* the product. Cuyana photographs the apartment, not the tote. Goop photographs the morning, not the supplement. Cheap photographs the product with a fingertip pointing at it. her75 IMG_6278 is the model — the woman taking a mirror selfie *with* the day-one sticker, not the sticker alone. *JeniFit rule: real-photo beats should imply a life, not demonstrate a feature.*

10. **One quiet hero per screen.** Premium screens have one place your eye lands. Cheap screens have 6 (sticker + headline + body + 3 CTAs + 2 footnotes + decorative sparkle). *JeniFit rule: every screen passes the squint test — can you name the one thing in one beat?*

## 3. Direction A — premium verdict

**Verdict: yes, with three load-bearing guardrails.** Without them this slips into Gen-Z mood board cheap within a build cycle, because mood boards seem easy ("just add a sticker") and the cheap variant has a *gravitational pull* toward exactly the wrong reference set (Pinterest, Depop, TikTok meme accounts).

**The three guardrails (non-negotiable for premium register):**

### Guardrail 1: One real-photo hero per screen, never a collage *of* stickers

The premium register of her75 is "one big real-life polaroid, full bleed, with one quiet typographic moment over or under it." It is NOT "5 stickers scattered around an empty canvas." When the founder shifts the hierarchy so the *stickers* become the visual mass instead of the *photo*, the register collapses to Pinterest mood board.

**Rule:** On any hero screen, the real photo occupies ≥40% of the visible canvas. Stickers occupy ≤10%. If a screen is sticker-heavy and photo-light, it is not a Direction A screen — it is a list screen with decoration, which is the cheap variant.

### Guardrail 2: Sticker count ≤ 2 per hero screen, sticker scale ≤ 60pt

Coquette stickers are accents. Three or more on one screen is the difference between "Cuyana editorial with one charm" and "TikTok edit with bow stickers everywhere." Aesop would ship one ribbon, period. The her75 IMG_6275 reference works because the stickers (book stack, bouquet, matcha) are *real photographed objects* arranged like a flat lay — they are not graphic stickers.

**Rule:** Maximum 2 stickers per screen. Stickers must be ≤60pt at @1x. Real-object stickers (a real photographed bow, a real photographed flower, a real photographed matcha) read MORE premium than illustrated stickers — borrow her75's actual technique: photograph small real objects on cream paper, cut out, layer.

### Guardrail 3: Photography sourced or shot to JeniFit's photo brief, never licensed stock

The cheap-vs-premium divide on real-photo apps is 100% sourcing. The lily bouquet in IMG_6275 reads premium because it looks like *somebody's actual Tuesday morning*. A stock photo of "young woman drinking smoothie in sunny kitchen" reads as femvertising even on a $200/yr app.

**Rule:** Either commission a 1-day photo shoot (model = real JeniFit user lookalike, props = real client objects, location = real apartment, not a studio), or curate ruthlessly from Are.na / It's Nice That / individual photographer Instagram (NEVER Unsplash or stock). Photo brief: warm natural light, slight grain, no Instagram filter, single subject, taken on a phone or a film camera that looks like a phone.

**If you can hold these three guardrails: Direction A is the correct premium register.** It lands JeniFit between her75 and Equilibria, which is exactly where the $47.99/yr price point belongs.

**If you can't hold these three guardrails: skip to Alternative B in §5.**

## 4. The coquette sticker question

**Verdict: reframe, don't retire.** The coquette sticker pack as it exists (AI-generated 3D plastic bows, gummy bears, disco balls) is correctly being killed. Those specific assets read TikTok-cheap because:
- 3D plastic-shader rendering reads as 2014 emoji-pack era
- AI generation has the tell-tale over-glossy "everything looks like a Bath & Body Works ornament" quality
- The motifs (disco ball especially) are 2022 Spotify Wrapped slop

**But the coquette *register* — small charming feminine objects as occasional accent — is correct for the brand.** This is the difference between *Sofia Coppola's Marie Antoinette* (premium coquette) and *Claire's Accessories* (cheap coquette). Both have bows. One reads as Cuyana, one reads as mall.

**The reframe:**

1. **Replace AI 3D stickers with photographed real objects.** A bow, a flower, a stamped envelope, a candle — actually photographed on cream paper, cut out, drop-shadowed. This is the her75 IMG_6275 technique. It costs one afternoon with a phone camera and a $30 bow from a craft store. Premium register lock.

2. **Cap at 2 per screen, accent only.** Never the visual mass. Never the hero. Always the eye-catcher on a primarily typographic or photographic composition.

3. **Specific, not generic.** A specific vintage Polly Pocket compact, a specific 1990s plastic phone charm, a specific dried rose. NOT generic "wellness charms." The specificity is what separates Goop's "this exact handmade ceramic mug from this exact artisan" from generic wellness clip art.

4. **Retire forever:** disco balls, sparkles (✨), gummy bears, anything plastic-rendered, anything pastel-airbrushed, anything that reads as 2022 Spotify Wrapped.

**Keep the *coquette identity*. Replace the *coquette execution*. The brand signal stays; the cheap render dies.**

## 5. Three alternative premium registers (if Direction A doesn't hold)

### Alternative A: Soft tactile editorial à la Aesop / Le Labo

**Aesthetic:** Cream + cocoa + sage. Single hero photo per screen. Photography style: warm natural light, single concrete object (ceramic mug, linen pillow, fresh herbs in glass), shot like a museum still life. Typography: Fraunces display, sparing italic, generous tracking. Zero stickers. Zero decoration.

**Reference brand:** Aesop product pages. The Class by Taryn Toomey. Cap Beauty's web.

**When to pick this:** If the photo-sourcing guardrail in Direction A is genuinely hard to hold (you can't shoot, you can't curate 30+ premium-grade real photos), strip stickers entirely and lean on photography + type only. This is the lowest-risk premium register because it removes the cheap variant entirely — there's nothing to slip toward.

**Tradeoff:** Loses some of JeniFit's *personality.* Reads slightly cold, like a Brooklyn skincare brand. Becomes harder to express the lowercase-casual voice without seeming inconsistent.

### Alternative B: Curated minimalism à la Cereal magazine / Kinfolk

**Aesthetic:** Off-white + black + one warm accent (terracotta or burnt sienna). Photography sparing — maybe one photo per 3 screens, otherwise pure typography on pristine canvas. Long-form copy treated as editorial body text. Margins generous, line-heights tall.

**Reference brand:** Cereal magazine, Kinfolk, Apartamento. Allbirds Tree Runner product detail pages.

**When to pick this:** If the brand wants to lean *harder* into "premium tool for serious women" and away from "warm Gen-Z friend." This is the closest register to what a $99/yr app would use. Probably too cold for JeniFit's actual cohort, but a useful counterweight to confirm Direction A is the right warmth.

**Tradeoff:** Loses TikTok-acquisition appeal entirely. Skews older. Probably leaves money on the table for the 22-26 segment.

### Alternative C: Modern personal-website craft à la Glossier early / Outdoor Voices

**Aesthetic:** Cream + cocoa + soft sage + one playful accent color used 5% of the time. Hand-drawn marks instead of stickers (a thin ink doodle of a bow, a hand-sketched flower, a checkmark drawn with a fountain pen). Type still Fraunces. Photography style: phone-camera real, not editorial.

**Reference brand:** Glossier 2014–2017 web. Outdoor Voices early. Maude.

**When to pick this:** If real-photo sourcing is the bottleneck AND you want to preserve the playful coquette personality. Hand-drawn marks are cheaper to produce, more on-brand than AI stickers, and naturally premium because they look human. (Apartamento covers, Wirth Research, half of Are.na lives in this register.)

**Tradeoff:** Requires hiring an illustrator OR finding a consistent ink-drawing AI prompt that doesn't look generated. Risk: looks twee if executed badly.

## 6. The Goop test

Hypothetical: a 32-year-old Goop newsletter reader, scrolling JeniFit's current onboarding in 2026. Does she read "premium tool I'd pay $48 for" or "TikTok app a niece would download"?

Five touchpoints, scored 1–5 (5 = Goop reader thinks this is premium):

| Touchpoint | Current JeniFit | Score | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| First screen | Italic Fraunces + cream + cocoa text + no body imagery | **4** | Reads editorial. Loses 1pt because "another wellness app first screen" — distinctive composition matters more than color palette. |
| Onboarding question screen | Tall pill options, cocoa CTA, generous whitespace | **3.5** | Functional and clean. Not yet *distinctive* — Goop reader can't tell this apart from BetterMe's question screens at a glance. Whitespace + Fraunces saves it. |
| Loading / analyzing screen | Animated %, italic Fraunces "Personalizing your space" register | **4.5** | her75-grade. The single most premium beat in the current set. |
| Paywall | Trial timeline + projection-as-hero + cream chrome | **3** | The price + plan layout is inherently transactional; premium signal comes from typography restraint. Current downside: anything that says "Save 72%" or "Most popular" pulls the score down 0.5pt. |
| Home (post-paywall) | Scrapbook chrome cards, italic Fraunces hero, sticker accent | **3.5–4** | The cocoa pill + Fraunces hero is premium; the sticker scatter risks dropping the score 0.5–1pt depending on density. |

**Aggregate Goop verdict (current state):** 3.7 / 5 → "this looks like a thoughtful wellness brand, I'd consider it." Not yet "I'd subscribe immediately," but in the *consideration set* of premium tools.

**Aggregate Goop verdict (Direction A executed with §3 guardrails):** 4.3 / 5 → "this is a premium tool. I trust this."

**Aggregate Goop verdict (Direction A WITHOUT §3 guardrails, sticker-heavy Pinterest variant):** 2.5 / 5 → "this is a TikTok app, my niece would like it."

The delta is real. The guardrails are doing 1.8 points of premium signal lift.

## 7. Final recommendation

**Ship Direction A with the three §3 guardrails. Do not ship Direction A without them.**

Specifically:

1. **Real-photo hero ≥40% canvas. Stickers ≤10% canvas. ≤2 stickers per screen.** Codify this as a design-system rule that survives the next 6 designers.

2. **Retire AI-generated 3D stickers permanently.** Replace with photographed real objects on cream paper, cut out, drop-shadowed (her75 technique). One afternoon, one craft store run, ~12 reusable assets.

3. **Establish a 30-photo "JeniFit photo library" before scaling Direction A.** Either commission a 1-day shoot or curate 30 hand-picked photos from individual photographer sources (Are.na boards, not Unsplash). If you can't commit to building this asset library, pivot to **Alternative A (Soft tactile editorial)** instead — it removes the photo dependency entirely.

4. **Treat the loading screen ("Personalizing your space" register) as the brand's North Star composition.** Every Direction A hero should aspire to that level of restraint. When in doubt, remove an element. When the choice is between adding a sticker or adding whitespace, add whitespace.

5. **Pressure-test against the Goop reader, not the TikTok scroller.** TikTok scroller is the acquisition channel; Goop reader is the *retention + price-justification* persona. Premium register is what makes $47.99/yr feel like a Cuyana tote, not a Five Below impulse buy. The brand has to read premium *after* she's paid, or churn happens at month 2.

**The risk to watch:** Direction A's failure mode is sticker creep. Every designer wants to add one more sticker. Six months in, every screen has 5 stickers and the brand has slipped to Pinterest. Codify the cap now or it will erode.

**The opportunity:** If JeniFit holds Direction A with discipline, it owns a clear visual space — *the editorial wellness app for women who'd rather use Cuyana than Lululemon*. That positioning is wide open. her75 owns it for 75-day-challenge culture; nobody owns it for ongoing weight-loss program category.

---

## TL;DR for the founder

- **Direction A is correct.** Real-photo hero + restrained sticker accent reads premium for $47.99/yr, on the right side of the her75 / Equilibria zone.
- **Three guardrails are non-negotiable:** one photo dominates each hero, ≤2 stickers per screen, no stock photography ever.
- **Kill the AI-generated 3D stickers permanently.** Reframe coquette as *photographed real small objects on cream paper* (her75 technique), not illustrated 3D charms.
- **Build the photo library before scaling.** 30 hand-picked or commissioned photos. If you can't commit, switch to Alternative A (Aesop register — pure typography + single-object hero).
- **The Goop test:** with guardrails 4.3/5, without guardrails 2.5/5. Discipline does 1.8 points of brand-equity lift.
- **The hidden danger:** sticker creep. Codify the cap now.
